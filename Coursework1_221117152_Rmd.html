As someone that runs and exercises a lot in my free time, I wanted to understand people’s interest in exercising and whether it has improved over the years. My time series is taken from Google Trends and gives information on the indexed volume of web search history for the term “Exercise” in GB from 2011 to 2025. First, plot the time series and model it using simple linear regression.

gb_exercise <- read.csv("C:/Users/rache/OneDrive/Desktop/Year 4/Sem 2 - Time series/Coursework1_221117152/GB_exercise_searchHistory.csv")
gb_exercise <- ts(gb_exercise$exercise, start = c(2011, 1), frequency = 12)

exercise_lm <- lm(gb_exercise ~ time(gb_exercise))
plot(gb_exercise, main = "Web search for exercise in GB", type = "l")
abline(exercise_lm, col = "red")

plot(residuals(exercise_lm), main = "Residuals of linear model", type = "l")

By plotting the search history of exercise, there are two key takeaways. The search volume has a seasonal pattern and search volume spiked during the covid-19 pandemic. Search volume is much higher at the start of the year and dips at the end of the year. The linear regression model is therefore not a good fit as it cannot capture any seasonal patterns. Next, we model the time series with the decompose function and explore the trend, seasonality, and residual components.

exercise_decom <- decompose(gb_exercise, type = "additive")

Trend component shows an gradual increase in search history for exercise from 2011 to 2020, followed by a spike in 2020 and 2021. Search history then drops back to pre-2020 levels in 2022 to 2025. The trend generally makes sense. The gradual increase in search history from 2011 to 2020 can be explained by the increase availability of mobile phones and technology. As mobile phones are more accessible, people are able to use technology in their every day lives for non-work purposes. The huge spike corresponds to the covid-19 pandemic, where people are stuck at home, hence have more time to consider exercise. The reduction is search history from 2022 onwards corresponds to the end to the covid-19 pandemic, where people are no longer working remotely and have less time to exercise.

plot(exercise_decom$trend, main = "Trend from the decomposed time series")

Seasonality component shows a clear decreasing trend in search history for exercise over the year. We can see steep reductions in the search history from April to August and from November to December. We expect the search history to be the higest in Janurary as most people try to loose weight after the holiday season and make new year resolutions during Janurary.

plot(exercise_decom$figure, main = "Seasonality from the decomposed time series", type = "l")

Resdiual component shows huge residuals in 2020 and 2021. This is expected as the covid-19 pandemic happened during this period and models cannot capture these “unexpected circumstances” unless we add an additional model specific for the pandemic. If we ignore the residuals from 2020 to 2021, the residuals look random.

plot(exercise_decom$random, main = "Resdiuals from the decomposed time series")

We can now test the properties of the residuals. Adf test is used to test if time series is stationary. Ljung-Box test is used to test if time series is white noise. The p-value for adf test is less than 5%, so residuals are stationary. However, the p-value for Ljung-Box test is less than 5%, indicating the residuals are not white noise.

#testing properties of residuals
exercise_decom_resid <- zoo::na.trim(exercise_decom$random)
#adf test
#H0: Time series is not stationary
tseries::adf.test(exercise_decom_resid) #p-value = 0.01 < 0.05

The pre-covid data predicts that search volume will display a decreasing trend over time. The decrease is driven by a reduction in search volume from 2017 to 2019.
